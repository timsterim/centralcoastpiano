The Work

Two Offerings, One Philosophy

Piano tuning and ear-playing instruction might seem like different worlds, but they share the same root: a deep listening. Whether coaxing a string into tune or unlocking a chord by feel, the ear is always the guide. You already have one — we just develop it together.

The Tuner

A Zen Practice in Sound

Tuning is a meditative act. The moment a piano moves from discordance into resonance is genuinely joyful — and that joy is present in every appointment. There's also the quiet delight of sitting down to play an instrument freshly brought to life, especially one never played before.

The Teacher

Playing What You Hear

Learning to play by ear is one of the most liberating things a musician can do. The approach connects especially well with kids — there's something natural about learning to trust what you hear before you read what you see. But this path is open to anyone at any age.

A well-tuned piano doesn't just sound better — it feels different to play. The resonance between strings, the way chords bloom and sustain, the confidence a player feels when the instrument responds truly. Tuning is the foundation everything else is built on.

  • 01
    The Standard Tune-Up

    For pianos that are tuned somewhat regularly, slightly out of pitch, or have a handful of keys with a tinny sound. A tuning in these cases will bring the instrument into its full resonance. This usually takes about two hours, done with care and genuine enjoyment. There is real satisfaction in handing a piano back to its owner in a state of harmony.

  • 02
    Pitch Raise

    When a piano has been out of tune for a long time or is significantly below pitch, it needs to be raised to tension before a fine tuning can hold. This is a two-pass process — bring the strings up, then tune them true. It takes more time, more skill, and rewards you with a piano that stays in tune much longer.

  • 03
    The Joy of a Fresh Instrument

    One of the quiet pleasures of this work is sitting down at the end of a session and playing the piano — especially one played for the first time. A freshly tuned piano has a kind of aliveness to it. You'll hear it too, the first time you play after a tuning. That difference is real, and it matters.

  • 04
    How Often Should You Tune?

    There are three service levels to consider. A pitch raise is for pianos that haven't been tuned in a long time — every key must be raised, sometimes twice, before it holds. A standard tune is for a piano tuned once every couple of years, where most keys need adjustment. A touch-up addresses a handful of strings that tend to go flat before the others, often due to an aging tuning pin — these are half price, and one is included within a year of a first tune with Central Coast Piano.

Ear playing is different from reading music, though they are not mutually exclusive. Knowing one enhances the knowing of the other. But those who carry the skills, knowledge and techniques to be an 'ear player' reap the rewards of not relying on sheet music and can have an almost unlimited set list. My students end up being the special few that can walk into a room with a piano and fill it with music and all its magic. Very early in the process they will be able to play accompaniment to support vocalists or instrument players — ukulele, guitar, any instrument — starting with Happy Birthday and ending with Christmas carols. Playing accompaniment is a great joy waiting to be had!

With even modest practice — 60 to 90 minutes a week, on your own terms — a student can start to play the bass, accompaniment, and melody of songs and put on a real show.

Music reading is woven in quietly throughout — students naturally learn to read notes and combine them with the chord symbols found in most scores.

Students are also able to play along with their favorite songs from their phone or a speaker. The Beatles are a particularly fun group to jam with!

These methods support composition too. Just the act of experimenting with different rhythm patterns combined with different chord progressions and a pinch of dynamics — and suddenly a song emerges.

  • 01
    Pitch Recognition, Major Scales and Chord Types

    After assessing the student's ability to discern which of two notes is higher in pitch, we begin with the foundations: major scales and chord types. The four most-used chord types are introduced — major, minor, and 7th chords. It is emphasized that learning these four basic chord types for a single note, such as C, means the student is empowered to play the same types for every other note on the piano. That unlocks 352 chords across all the keys. Solfege is introduced through Do-Re-Mi from The Sound of Music, and fingering is demonstrated as a path to simplicity.

  • 02
    Ear Playing Theory and Chord Inversions

    Students learn that most songs can be played with just 3 or 4 chords, and that many follow a 1-4-5 pattern. This opens the door to chord inversions — the same chord voiced in three or more positions across an octave.

  • 03
    Rhythm and Hand Differential Skills

    Fun drills introduce the student to using both hands with differing rhythm patterns. Fingering begins to be emphasized for both scales and inversions, and the distinct fingering for multi-octave scales is introduced.

  • 04
    Deconstructing Songs by Ear

    This is where it gets exciting. We take songs most students already know — Happy Birthday, The Alphabet Song (same melody as Twinkle Twinkle Little Star), Brahms' Lullaby, The Lion Sleeps Tonight, Heart and Soul, and seasonal favorites like Jingle Bells and Silent Night — and learn to hear and play them using the 1-4-5 pattern.

    For Kids & Adults Alike

    There's something about the ear-playing approach that connects naturally with kids — it meets them where music already lives in their bodies, before notation adds complexity. But adults thrive here too. If you've always wanted to just play without needing sheet music, this is your path. You're more ready than you think.

  • 05
    Augmented and Diminished Chords, Minor Scales

    More complex chord types are introduced and made approachable, and creativity gets a big boost with the introduction of minor scales.

    Dynamics: Giving Expression to Your Soul

    The feelings evoked by playing forte (loud) versus piano (soft), a change of tempo, modulation (changing key), shifting octaves, sustains and staccatos — all of these can be arranged to paint the canvas of your very being in a way like no other. Music is magic, and this final layer makes you a magician.
